For my masters I've studied some of the oldest stars that we know of so far. These stars belong to the central regions of the galaxy and have on average 100 to 1000 times less heave elements compared to the Sun. For my project I got to measure the abundances of 4 elements that form through the neutron capture process, strontium (Sr), ytrium (Y), barium (Ba) and europium (Eu). The point of the project was to get a better picture of how these elements are formed in the early Universe. Stars in general act as fossil records to great degree. If one can measure the abundances of elements in a star's atmosphere, we can then get a detailed picture of the gas cloud this star formed from. That's what we did with these old stars. By measuring the abundances of these elements and comparing them to theoretical models of the first stars death. We can set constraints on the nature of the first stars, how massive they were? how did they die? and what elements did they form more effocoently?

My supervisor Louise Howes is studying the light elements in these stars. The plan is to publish a paper detailing the results that we found.
