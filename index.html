My research!

For my masters I've studied some of the oldest stars discovered so far. These stars belong to the central regions of the galaxy and have on average 100 to 1000 times less heavy elements compared to the Sun. For my project I got to measure how abundant 4 heavy elements are in these stars. The elements are strontium (Sr), ytrium (Y), barium (Ba) and europium (Eu). These elements form through a process termed the neutron capture process. The point of the project is to get a better picture of how these elements are formed in the early Universe. Stars in general act as fossil records to a great degree. If one can measure the abundances of elements in a star's atmosphere, we can then get a detailed picture of the gas cloud this star formed from. That's what we did with these old stars. I measured the abundances of these elements and compared them to theoretical models of how the first stars might've died. From this we can set constraints on the nature of the first stars, how massive they were? how did they die? and what elements did they form more efficiently?

My supervisor Louise Howes is studying the light elements in these stars. Having a detailed understanding of the chemistry of the oldest stars would get us a step closer to understanding the nature of the first stars that ever formed in the Universe. Those stars changed the Universe from a dark featureless one to the marvelous creation we observe today.
